\section{Related Works}
Prior review literature on Small Language Models (SLMs) and SLM--LLM collaboration can be grouped into four highly relevant works that overlap with our scope at different levels of methodology and emphasis. The closest methodological competitor is \emph{State of the Art and Future Directions of Small Language Models: A Systematic Review}, a PRISMA 2020-guided SLR that sources studies from multiple databases and provides a reproducible selection protocol~\cite{slmreview2025prisma}. While strong in transparency, it is broader in topic focus (SLMs in general) and its coverage window ends around early 2025, limiting visibility into the 2025--2026 wave of hybrid and agent-based systems.

The remaining three works are narrative surveys with substantial thematic overlap. \emph{A Comprehensive Survey of Small Language Models in the Era of LLMs} offers wide coverage of SLM definitions, training techniques, applications, collaboration with LLMs, and trustworthiness, but it does not follow a systematic and reproducible selection protocol~\cite{wang2024slmsurvey}. \emph{A Survey on Collaborative Mechanisms Between Large and Small Language Models} provides a technically detailed taxonomy of collaboration patterns (e.g., routing, auxiliary modules, fusion, and distillation) with an emphasis on edge-device requirements, yet its analysis is primarily mechanism-centered rather than corpus-centered~\cite{chen2025collabmech}. Finally, \emph{A Survey on Collaborating Small and Large Language Models for Performance, Cost-effectiveness, Cloud-edge Privacy, and Trustworthiness} overlaps strongly with our themes (performance, cost, privacy, trustworthiness) but remains a narrative survey without a formal PRISMA-guided study selection and extraction schema~\cite{wang2025collabperf}.

Across these reviews, the gap is not a lack of attention to SLMs or collaboration, but a difference in analytical emphasis and evidential grounding. Existing surveys typically answer what kinds of models or mechanisms exist; they less often align evidence across domains and report comparable operational metrics. In contrast, our study is positioned as a PRISMA-guided, engineering-oriented synthesis that (i) treats domain suitability as a first-class axis, (ii) maps research questions to extraction fields so claims remain traceable, and (iii) emphasizes quantitative indicators (accuracy, latency, cost, and energy) to support deployment-relevant decisions~\cite{slmreview2025prisma}.
