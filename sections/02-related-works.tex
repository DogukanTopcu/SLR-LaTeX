\section{Related Works}

Prior review literature on Small Language Models (SLMs) and SLM--LLM collaboration can be grouped into four highly relevant works that overlap with our scope at different levels of methodology and emphasis. The closest study is a recent PRISMA 2020-guided SLR that sources studies from multiple databases and provides a reproducible selection protocol~\cite{slmreview2025prisma}. While strong in transparency, it adopts a broader topical scope (SLMs in general), and its coverage window ends around early 2025, limiting visibility into the 2025--2026 wave of hybrid and agent-based systems.

The remaining three works are narrative surveys with substantial thematic overlap. One study provides wide coverage of SLM definitions, training techniques, applications, collaboration with LLMs, and trustworthiness, but does not follow a systematic and reproducible selection protocol~\cite{wang2024slmsurvey}. Another presents a technically detailed taxonomy of collaboration patterns (e.g., routing, auxiliary modules, fusion, and distillation) with an emphasis on edge-device requirements, yet its analysis is primarily mechanism-centered rather than corpus-centered~\cite{chen2025collabmech}. A further work overlaps strongly with our themes (performance, cost, privacy, and trustworthiness) but remains a narrative survey without a formal PRISMA-guided study selection and extraction schema~\cite{wang2025collabperf}.

Across these reviews, the gap is not a lack of attention to SLMs or collaboration, but a difference in analytical emphasis and evidential grounding. Existing surveys typically describe what kinds of models or mechanisms exist; however, they rarely align empirical evidence across domains or report comparable operational metrics. In contrast, our study is positioned as a PRISMA-guided, engineering-oriented synthesis that (i) treats domain suitability as a first-class axis, (ii) maps research questions to structured extraction fields to ensure traceability, and (iii) emphasizes quantitative indicators (accuracy, latency, cost, and energy) to support deployment-relevant decision-making.
