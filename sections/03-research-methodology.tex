\section{Research Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Research Questions}
\label{sec:research_questions}

To systematically evaluate the technical and operational trade-offs between monolithic, hybrid, and multi-agent architectures, the review protocol is structured around the following four core research questions (RQs):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Performance):} How does the performance (accuracy/efficacy) of Multi-agent SLMs or Hybrid Architectures (LLM + SLM) compare to single Monolithic LLMs in specific reasoning and classification tasks?
    
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Efficiency):} What is the impact of Hybrid and Multi-agent architectures on computational efficiency, specifically regarding inference cost, latency, and energy consumption, compared to Monolithic models?
    
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Orchestration):} What orchestration and routing mechanisms are employed to manage the division of labor between large and small models in hybrid systems?
    
    \item \textbf{RQ4 (Domain Suitability):} In which specific domains (e.g., Healthcare, Coding, Mathematics) do SLMs achieve performance comparable to LLMs, and where do they fail (e.g., long-context reasoning)?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Information Sources and Search Strategy}
\label{sec:search_strategy}

To capture a comprehensive and high-quality corpus of contemporary language model architectures, the literature search was conducted utilizing the Web of Science (WoS) Core Collection. Utilizing WoS as the primary search engine allowed for the retrieval of indexed peer-reviewed journal articles, conference proceedings, and highly cited preprints within a single dataset.

The search strategy was systematically developed by extracting the core concepts from the defined research questions and refining the terminology through initial pilot searches. This iterative process helped identify common synonyms and alternate phrasing used in the literature (e.g., ``Compact Model'' for SLMs, or ``Agentic'' for multi-agent systems). To guarantee that every retrieved study examined the intersection of model size, architectural design, and system evaluation, the final query was structured into four mandatory components. The following Boolean query was applied to the title, abstract, and keyword fields:

\begin{quote}
( "Large Language Model*" OR "LLM" OR "Generative AI" OR "Foundation Model*" ) AND 
( "Small Language Model*" OR "SLM" OR "Compact Model*" OR "Lightweight Model*" ) AND 
( "Multi-agent" OR "Hybrid" OR "Orchestration" OR "Routing" OR "Ensemble" OR "Agentic" ) AND 
( "Performance" OR "Accuracy" OR "Efficiency" OR "Latency" OR "Cost" OR "Benchmark" )
\end{quote}

\subsection{Study Selection Process}
\label{sec:study_selection}

The study selection process strictly adhered to the PRISMA 2020 guidelines \cite{page2021prisma}. The progression of the complete screening funnel, detailing records identified, screened, sought, and assessed, is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:prisma_flowchart}.

The initial literature search conducted via the Web of Science Core Collection yielded 116 records. As the dataset was generated from a single database, zero duplicate records required removal before the screening commenced. 

The selection process was executed in two systematic phases. During the initial Identification and Screening phase, the title, abstract, and keywords of all 116 records were manually reviewed. At this stage, 32 records were excluded for failing to meet primary eligibility criteria, such as being non-technical surveys or operating in unrelated domains (e.g., computer vision pipeline optimization). Following this phase, 84 reports were identified for full-text retrieval. Of these, 4 reports were not successfully retrieved due to strict paywall constraints or being published in unindexed workshop proceedings, leaving 80 reports advancement for depth evaluation.

During the final Assessment phase, the 80 retrieved reports underwent a thorough full-text methodology review. This phase resulted in the exclusion of 26 additional records against specific technical constraints. The primary reasons for exclusion during full-text review were: (1) Model size or architecture mismatch (e.g., evaluated models exceeded the strict $\le$ 7B parameter limit or lacked definitive SLM+LLM collaborative interaction) (n=18); (2) Topic or scope out of range (n=5); and (3) Lack of original empirical data or being a narrative survey not previously caught (n=3). 

To ensure objectivity throughout the selection process, a team consensus mechanism was utilized. Inclusion and exclusion decisions at both stages were made via majority vote of the authors, with any conflicting tie resolved through collaborative discussion and escalation to the academic advisors. Following this rigorous selection pipeline, a final corpus of 54 primary studies was established for data extraction and synthesis.

\subsection{Data Extraction and Synthesis Strategy}
\label{sec:data_extraction}

To systematically address the research questions, a standardized data extraction protocol was applied to the final corpus of 54 primary studies. For each study, data was extracted into a structured matrix to capture both the functional design of the systems and the empirical evidence used to justify them. 

The extracted fields included the specific SLM and LLM models utilized, the overarching architecture type (e.g., Hybrid, Multi-Agent, RAG, Ensemble), orchestration mechanisms, evaluation metrics, baseline models, and quantitative performance indicators (accuracy, latency, cost, and energy). To ensure analytical rigor, each extracted variable was explicitly mapped to one or more of the core Research Questions (RQs). This mapping is detailed in Table \ref{tab:rq-matrix}.

Following data extraction, a narrative synthesis approach was utilized. Because the included studies operate across diverse domains (e.g., telecommunications, healthcare, software engineering) and report varying metrics, a purely statistical meta-analysis was not feasible. Instead, studies were grouped by their architectural taxonomy, allowing for a comparative analysis of how different orchestration methods and domain constraints impact the performance and efficiency trade-offs between large and small models.
