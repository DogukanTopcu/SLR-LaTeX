\subsection{Information Sources and Search Strategy}
\label{sec:search_strategy}

To capture a comprehensive and high-quality corpus of contemporary AI architectures, the literature search was conducted utilizing the Web of Science (WoS) Core Collection. Utilizing WoS as the primary search engine allowed for the retrieval of indexed peer-reviewed journal articles, conference proceedings, and highly cited preprints within a single dataset. The search timeframe was restricted to publications between 2023 and 2026 to reflect the rapid, recent emergence of small and agentic language models.

The search strategy was designed to capture the intersection of model size, architectural design, and system evaluation. The following Boolean query was applied to the title, abstract, and keyword fields:

\begin{quote}
( "Large Language Model*" OR "LLM" OR "Generative AI" OR "Foundation Model*" ) AND 
( "Small Language Model*" OR "SLM" OR "Compact Model*" OR "Lightweight Model*" OR "TinyLlama" OR "Phi-3" OR "Mistral" OR "Qwen" ) AND 
( "Multi-agent" OR "Hybrid" OR "Orchestration" OR "Routing" OR "Ensemble" OR "Agentic" ) AND 
( "Performance" OR "Accuracy" OR "Efficiency" OR "Latency" OR "Cost" OR "Benchmark" )
\end{quote}

\subsection{Study Selection Process}
\label{sec:study_selection}

The study selection process adhered to the PRISMA 2020 guidelines \cite{page2021prisma}. The initial literature search yielded 116 records. Because the export was generated from a single, centralized database engine (Web of Science), zero duplicate records required manual removal. The progression of the screening process is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:slr-process}.

The screening was conducted in two distinct phases to ensure methodological rigor. During the initial Title and Abstract screening, 22 records were excluded for lacking general relevance to the architectural scope. These exclusions primarily consisted of non-empirical narrative surveys, hardware scheduling studies lacking architectural orchestration, and studies operating in entirely separate domains (e.g., computer vision or 3D hand gesture recognition). 

The remaining 94 records advanced to a rigorous Full-Text review. During this phase, an additional 40 studies were excluded after deeply evaluating their methodologies against the technical constraints. Exclusions at this stage occurred when the evaluated ``small'' models exceeded the strict $\le$ 7B parameter threshold, the evaluations relied on closed-box APIs that hindered reproducibility, or the methodologies failed to report quantitative system-level performance metrics. 

To mitigate selection bias during both screening phases, a team consensus mechanism was utilized. Inclusion and exclusion decisions were made via majority vote among the reviewing authors. In the event of a tie, the conflict was resolved through collaborative discussion and escalation to the academic advisors. Following this two-stage screening process, a final corpus of 54 primary studies was established for data extraction and synthesis.

\subsection{Data Extraction and Synthesis Strategy}
\label{sec:data_extraction}

To systematically address the research questions, a standardized data extraction protocol was applied to the final corpus of 54 primary studies. For each study, data was extracted into a structured matrix to capture both the functional design of the systems and the empirical evidence used to justify them. 

The extracted fields included the specific SLM and LLM models utilized, the overarching architecture type (e.g., Hybrid, Multi-Agent, RAG, Ensemble), orchestration mechanisms, evaluation metrics, baseline models, and quantitative performance indicators (accuracy, latency, cost, and energy). To ensure analytical rigor, each extracted variable was explicitly mapped to one or more of the core Research Questions (RQs). This mapping is detailed in Table \ref{tab:rq-matrix}.

Following data extraction, a narrative synthesis approach was utilized. Because the included studies operate across diverse domains (e.g., telecommunications, healthcare, software engineering) and report varying metrics, a purely statistical meta-analysis was not feasible. Instead, studies were grouped by their architectural taxonomy, allowing for a comparative analysis of how different orchestration methods and domain constraints impact the performance and efficiency trade-offs between large and small models.
