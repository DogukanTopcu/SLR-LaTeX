\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) excel at reasoning, coding, and multimodal tasks~\cite{olfactory2025,staticanalysis2025,egi2025}. However, their substantial hardware and deployment requirements often confine them to cloud infrastructure or high-end local systems, creating challenges for developers concerned with privacy, cloud dependence, and operational cost~\cite{energytoken2025,qwqrouting2025,tigani2025cpu,aydin2025integrated}.

In this context, small language models (SLMs) have emerged as an attractive alternative. Their ability to run locally supports data sovereignty, deployment autonomy, and lower cost, while fine-tuning and distillation have enabled competitive performance in domain-specific tasks~\cite{xie2025fusing,zhu2024distilling,rethinkllm2025}. SLMs also integrate effectively with explicit control mechanisms such as routing, verification thresholds, and planners, improving controllability in practical systems~\cite{aydin2025integrated,qwqrouting2025,ji2025raprag}. However, they still lag behind frontier LLMs in broad reasoning and tasks requiring extensive contextual knowledge~\cite{egi2025,dorfner2024comparing}. As a result, recent research increasingly examines not only whether SLMs can replace LLMs, but also when hybrid or agent-based architectures offer a better capability--efficiency trade-off~\cite{hao2024hybrid,shao2025dot,multiagent2025}.

Existing studies show that this is primarily a systems design question. Across domains, hybrid and multi-agent architectures aim to improve not only accuracy, but also latency, cost, and computational efficiency through mechanisms such as edge--cloud verification, expert routing, retrieval, and planner-based orchestration~\cite{hao2024hybrid,lee2024orchestrallm,alabbasi2024teleoracle,ji2025raprag,chatzistefanidis2025symbiotic,han2025chest,energytoken2025}. These developments motivate a closer examination of when a single SLM is sufficient, when an LLM remains necessary, and when collaborative architectures provide the most effective balance between capability and efficiency.

Accordingly, this review analyzes when a single SLM is sufficient, when an LLM remains necessary, and when hybrid or multi-agent architectures provide the best trade-off between capability and efficiency. It further examines the domain conditions under which fine-tuned SLMs can realistically replace or complement larger models, and where they continue to fall short.

The main contributions of this review are as follows: (1) it synthesizes 54 studies covering monolithic LLMs, hybrid SLM--LLM architectures, fine-tuned SLMs, and multi-agent workflows; (2) it reframes the literature around four practical research questions: performance, efficiency, orchestration, and domain suitability; and (3) it provides an analytical review matrix mapping each study to the research question(s) it addresses.
