
\section{Introduction}
Large language models (LLMs) excel at reasoning, coding, and multimodal tasks~\cite{olfactory2025,staticanalysis2025,egi2025}. However, due to the high hardware requirements (RAM, GPU, etc.) they demand, these models are usually run only on cloud-based infrastructures or very advanced local systems~\cite{energytoken2025,qwqrouting2025}. This creates a significant bottleneck for system developers seeking to reduce cloud dependency, ensure data privacy, and avoid high operational costs~\cite{tigani2025cpu,aydin2025integrated}.

As a result of these limitations, small language models (SLMs) are emerging as a strong alternative. 
Their ability to run on local machines provides a significant advantage in terms of data security and deployment autonomy, while also offering substantially lower operational costs than cloud-scale LLM setups~\cite{tigani2025cpu,aydin2025integrated}. 
Moreover, recent studies show that SLMs can be adapted to specific domains via fine-tuning or distillation, enabling competitive performance in specialized tasks~\cite{xie2025fusing,zhu2024distilling,rethinkllm2025}. 
In practical systems, SLMs are also attractive because they integrate well with explicit control modules (e.g., rule-based filters, routing/verification thresholds, or planners), improving controllability and reliability beyond prompt-only solutions~\cite{aydin2025integrated,qwqrouting2025,ji2025raprag}. 
However, SLMs still struggle to match the broad expertise and robust reasoning of massive frontier models, especially on tasks requiring deep contextual understanding and extensive world knowledge~\cite{egi2025,dorfner2024comparing}. 
Therefore, framing the problem as a simple ``SLM-versus-LLM'' competition is no longer sufficient; instead, current research investigates when a single SLM is adequate, when an LLM is necessary, and when hybrid or agent-based architectures are preferable~\cite{hao2024hybrid,shao2025dot,multiagent2025}.

The existing literature provides concrete, domain-specific examples showing that the choice between SLMs, LLMs, and hybrid designs is primarily a systems decision. In telecommunications, Symbiotic Agents reports up to a fivefold reduction in decision error and near-real-time operation with up to 99.9\% lower GPU overhead using SLM variants~\cite{chatzistefanidis2025symbiotic}. In medicine, self-reflective pipelines pair a compact generator with an LLM-based judge to iteratively refine outputs and improve diagnostic reliability~\cite{han2025chest}.

These developments provide practical alternatives for scenarios where relying on a single monolithic LLM is either insufficient for task demands or impractical under strict resource constraints. Accordingly, recent work increasingly targets measurable improvements beyond raw accuracy, including reduced computational load, lower operational cost, and tighter latency budgets~\cite{hao2024hybrid,qwqrouting2025,chatzistefanidis2025symbiotic,energytoken2025}. Across domains, researchers have reported which hybrid design patterns are effective for specific task classes---for example, selective edge--cloud verification, expert routing, and retrieval- or planner-centered pipelines---and how these choices translate to real-world deployments~\cite{hao2024hybrid,lee2024orchestrallm,alabbasi2024teleoracle,ji2025raprag}.

Motivated by these trends, this review analyzes when a single SLM is sufficient, when an LLM remains necessary, and when hybrid or multi-agent architectures provide the best trade-off between capability and efficiency. We further examine domain conditions under which fine-tuned SLMs can realistically replace or complement larger models, and where they still fall short, synthesizing recurring architectural motifs across telecom, medical, and cybersecurity settings.

The main contributions of this review are as follows: (1) It provides a synthesized overview of 54 studies covering cloud-based monolithic LLMs, hybrid SLM--LLM architectures, the performance of fine-tuned SLMs versus large models, and multi-agent workflows. (2) It reframes the literature around four distinct practical research questions: Performance, Efficiency, Architecture, and Domain Suitability. (3) It creates an analytical review matrix that reveals the commonalities and differences among the studies, mapping which specific research question each study addresses.
