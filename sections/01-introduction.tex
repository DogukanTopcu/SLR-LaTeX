\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) excel at reasoning, coding, and multimodal tasks~\cite{olfactory2025,staticanalysis2025,egi2025}. However, their substantial hardware and deployment requirements often confine them to cloud infrastructure or high-end local systems, creating challenges for developers concerned with privacy, cloud dependence, and operational cost~\cite{energytoken2025,qwqrouting2025,tigani2025cpu,aydin2025integrated}.

In this context, small language models (SLMs) have emerged as an attractive alternative. Their ability to run locally supports data sovereignty, deployment autonomy, and lower cost, while fine-tuning and distillation have enabled competitive performance in domain-specific tasks~\cite{xie2025fusing,zhu2024distilling,rethinkllm2025}. SLMs also integrate effectively with explicit control mechanisms such as routing, verification thresholds, and planners, improving controllability in practical systems~\cite{aydin2025integrated,qwqrouting2025,ji2025raprag}. However, they still lag behind frontier LLMs in broad reasoning and tasks requiring extensive contextual knowledge~\cite{egi2025,dorfner2024comparing}. As a result, recent research increasingly examines how small and large models can be combined so that hybrid or agent-based architectures offer a better capability--efficiency trade-off than full monolithic LLM inference~\cite{hao2024hybrid,shao2025dot,multiagent2025}.

Existing studies show that this is primarily a systems design question. Across domains, hybrid and multi-agent architectures aim to improve not only accuracy, but also latency, cost, and computational efficiency through mechanisms such as edge--cloud verification, expert routing, retrieval, and planner-based orchestration~\cite{hao2024hybrid,lee2024orchestrallm,alabbasi2024teleoracle,ji2025raprag,chatzistefanidis2025symbiotic,han2025chest,energytoken2025}. These developments motivate a closer examination of how collaborative architectures allocate work between small and large models, when monolithic LLM baselines remain necessary, and when orchestration provides the most effective balance between capability and efficiency.

Accordingly, this review analyzes when hybrid or multi-agent SLM--LLM architectures provide a better trade-off between capability and efficiency than monolithic LLM baselines. It further examines the domain conditions under which routing, retrieval, planning, and role specialization allow smaller models to contribute effectively within collaborative pipelines, and where those pipelines continue to fall short.

The main contributions of this review are as follows: (1) it synthesizes 54 studies covering hybrid SLM--LLM architectures, retrieval-centered collaboration, ensembles, and multi-agent workflows, using monolithic LLMs as comparative baselines where reported; (2) it reframes the literature around four practical research questions: performance, efficiency, orchestration, and domain suitability; and (3) it provides an analytical review matrix mapping each study to the research question(s) it addresses.
