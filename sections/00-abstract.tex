\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) deliver strong reasoning and generation performance, but their cost, latency, energy demand, and hardware requirements remain substantial. Small language models (SLMs) are better suited to edge and privacy-sensitive deployment, yet their capabilities vary across domains. This paper presents a systematic literature review of 54 studies on collaborative SLM--LLM systems spanning hybrid, multi-agent, retrieval-centered, and ensemble architectures, with monolithic LLMs treated as baselines rather than as a primary architecture class. Guided by four research questions on performance, efficiency, orchestration, and domain suitability, we apply a PRISMA 2020-based review design to synthesize models, architectures, datasets, metrics, latency, cost, energy, and reported limitations. The literature shows a clear shift toward hybrid and multi-agent designs, but cost and energy remain far less reported than accuracy. Overall, SLMs are most competitive when they are paired with routing, retrieval, planning, or fusion mechanisms in narrow, structured, or domain-specific settings.

\end{abstract}
