\section{Findings}
The findings are organized below according to the four research questions defined in the review protocol.
\subsection{RQ1: Comparative Performance of Hybrid and Multi-Agent Systems}

The review shows that both hybrid and multi-agent architectures can outperform standalone small-model baselines, but their advantages emerge through different forms of coordination rather than parameter scaling alone. Hybrid systems achieve their strongest gains when computation is selectively distributed between local small models and stronger remote models. Token-level edge--cloud collaboration improves small-model performance without requiring full-query escalation: one study reports LLM-comparable quality on GSM8K at only 25.8\% of full-cloud cost by using TinyLlama 1.1B or Llama-2-7B as edge-side drafting models and Llama-2-70B as a cloud verifier (S2). Similarly, routing only about 7\% of low-confidence tokens from a 0.5B on-device model to a cloud-hosted Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct model yields over 60\% performance improvement over the local-only setup while preserving more than 80\% cost reduction relative to full LLM inference (S46). This pattern is reinforced by uncertainty-aware hybrid inference, where opportunistic skipping reduces uplink transmissions and LLM computations by 45.93\%, reaches up to 97.54\% of LLM inference accuracy, and delivers 2.54$\times$ faster token throughput than hybrid inference without skipping (S45). At the sub-task level, structured task decomposition and cloud--edge allocation reduce average reasoning time by 66.12\% and API cost by 83.57\% while maintaining reasoning accuracy comparable to the best baselines (S53). In domain-specific settings, hybrid orchestration can also outperform larger general-purpose models: a fine-tuned Phi-2 (2.7B) combined with retrieval, reranking, hybrid search, and long-context support achieves 84.20\% accuracy, exceeding GPT-4o mini (82.05\%) and GPT-4o (80.80\%) on telecom question answering (S4).

Multi-agent systems, by contrast, show their comparative strength when performance depends on role specialization, critique, verification, and distributed reasoning. A three-agent Phi-3-based architecture performs on par with human experts in conceptual genomics tasks, although its gains weaken on more complex code-generation workflows, indicating that multi-agent benefits remain task-sensitive (S51). In reasoning-intensive question answering, a Proponent--Opponent--Arbitrator structure built on compact models such as Qwen2.5 0.5B and DeepSeek-R1 1.5B surpasses both single-agent reasoning and vanilla LLM baselines with only 1.8$\times$ inference-time overhead (S37). Likewise, assistant--checker collaboration supported by memory and feedback loops enables small-model multi-agent systems to approach or exceed larger baselines across several tasks: LeptoConnect-7B outperforms GPT-3.5-turbo by 163.1\% on database tasks, while LeptoConnect-1.8B reaches 81.6\% of GPT-4's score on knowledge graph tasks, and an enhanced TinyLlama-1.8B improves ROUGE-L by 63.4\% over CodeLlama-7B in code correction (S20). Overall, the findings indicate that hybrid systems are most effective when the main challenge is balancing accuracy with latency and cost through adaptive routing, whereas multi-agent systems are most effective when the task benefits from explicit decomposition of reasoning roles and iterative verification. Thus, the comparative performance advantage of both architecture families is driven primarily by orchestration strategy, not by model size alone.
\subsection{RQ2: Efficiency, Cost, Latency, and Energy Trade-offs}

The review reveals that while efficiency motivates the adoption of hybrid and multi-agent systems, the actual impact on cost, latency, and energy depends heavily on the orchestration method. Hybridization is not a universal efficiency solution; it introduces a trade-off space where computational bottlenecks are often shifted rather than eliminated.

Regarding inference cost and token consumption, architectures employing selective escalation or task decomposition drastically reduce reliance on massive standalone LLMs. S46 achieves an over 80\% cost reduction compared to full LLM inference by routing fewer than 7\% of generated tokens to the cloud. Similarly, S2 uses a token-level draft-verification method to match the quality of a 70B parameter LLM while consuming only 25.8\% to 31.2\% of standard cloud inference costs. Query-level decomposition yields similar savings: S53 cuts average API costs by 83.57\% via parallel reasoning. Computational savings are also heavily emphasized through token reduction. S33 achieves a 95\% reduction in LLM token transmissions via federated uncertainty-aware routing, and S27 eliminates redundant multi-round communications through an SLM-generated directed acyclic graph, reducing token consumption by 35\% to 70\%. In dialogue state tracking, S6 highlights that a retrieval-based router reduces overall computational FLOPs by approximately 62\%.

Conversely, the impact on latency is highly polarized. In optimal scenarios, relying on lightweight models accelerates throughput. A fine-tuned SLM in a hybrid classification pipeline (S32) operates at 7.41 ms on a GPU, making it over 20 times faster than its larger counterpart. Furthermore, S45 shows that uncertainty-aware skipping in an edge-cloud setup provides 2.54$\times$ faster token throughput. However, orchestration mechanisms frequently introduce new latency bottlenecks. Planners and retrievers add non-trivial temporal overhead; the adaptive retrieval planner in S10 takes 1.42 seconds compared to 0.95 seconds for naive retrieval, and S4 explicitly identifies cross-encoder reranking as its primary latency bottleneck. Multi-agent collaboration also inherently multiplies inference cycles. S37 reports a 1.8$\times$ inference-time overhead for adversarial debate, and complex agentic workflows in S22 can push average response latencies to 107 seconds. Processing time is further impacted by hardware and modality constraints, such as constrained local hardware (S11), dual-path video sampling (S5), CPU-only zero-shot ensembles taking upwards of 859 seconds (S47), and multi-turn key-value cache recomputation at the edge (S48).

Energy consumption remains the least documented metric in the reviewed studies, and most reported efficiency gains are resource proxies rather than direct energy measurements. The clearest energy-centered evidence comes from S34, which argues for energy-per-token as a more informative metric and shows that forcing a small model to execute complex reasoning workflows, such as Chain-of-Thought, can substantially increase its per-query energy use. In some settings, a larger model prompted more directly is therefore more energy-efficient than a smaller model that generates a long reasoning trace. By contrast, S20 reports a 26.4\% reduction in computational redundancy through dual-scale memory, which may indicate lower resource demand but does not by itself constitute a direct energy benchmark. Taken together, the review suggests that energy efficiency depends on orchestration and generation length as much as on model size, and that stronger claims require standardized, hardware-specific measurement protocols.

\subsection{RQ3: Orchestration and Routing Mechanisms}
The studies contains a broad set of orchestration mechanisms, but they can be organized into a small number of recurring families. Across the included studies, performance differences are explained less by absolute model size than by how effectively the system retrieves evidence, routes uncertainty, and divides labor across components.

Token-level orchestration is most visible in verification-oriented hybrid systems. S2 uses the SLM as a drafter and the LLM as a verifier on difficult tokens. S45 extends this pattern with uncertainty-aware skipping so that likely acceptable SLM tokens can bypass the remote model. S46 reaches a related design point by routing only low-confidence tokens for external inference. Taken together, these studies show that token-level collaboration provides fine-grained control over answer quality, but it can also introduce communication and synchronization overhead when escalation rates increase.

Query-level routing appears when the system selects a processing path for the full input rather than for individual tokens. S6 exemplifies retrieval-based expert routing at the dialogue level, while S32 shows that instance-level model selection can also be effective in customer-review analysis. These designs are most useful when ``easy'' and ``hard'' cases can be separated in a semantically meaningful way before full generation begins.

RAG is a major control mechanism across the included studies even when the architecture is classified as hybrid rather than purely RAG. S4, S10, S31, and S44 use retrieval not only to expand context but also to constrain the action space of a smaller model. In these studies, retrieval acts as a coordination layer that narrows ambiguity, grounds the answer in domain evidence, and reduces reliance on raw model scale alone.

Task decomposition is increasingly important in the recent literature. S53 uses a task decomposer, dependency graph, and plug-and-play adapter to allocate subtasks between local and remote models. S52 uses a feedback-integrated workflow that gathers structured feedback from multiple perspectives before revising the prompt of the smaller model. Related role-based decomposition also appears in S11, S20, and S51. Across these studies, the common idea is that small models remain viable when the workflow can be partitioned into specialist stages rather than handled as a single undifferentiated generation step.

Orchestration frequently extends beyond unimodal text processing to integrate diverse data modalities and specialized neural architectures. Several frameworks are designed to fuse language models with non-text representations and structured data. For example, S5 combines coarse-grained and fine-grained visual semantics through prompt-guided video sampling, while S3 fuses language-model embeddings with graph-convolutional features for molecular-property prediction. This heterogeneous routing is particularly prevalent in applied domains such as medicine; S17 orchestrates convolutional networks (ConvNeXt) for anatomical localization alongside a self-reflective LLM agent, and S25 employs a late-fusion approach to combine structured gradient boosting models (EHR tabular data) with unstructured clinical text analysis. In these settings, orchestration operates as a structured fusion mechanism, ensuring that visual, spatial, or mathematical data is processed by the optimal specialized architecture before language generation occurs.

\subsection{RQ4: Domain Suitability and Application-Specific Constraints}
The review reveals that the choice between hybrid and multi-agent systems is rarely domain-agnostic. The overarching constraints of the operational environment---whether they be strict privacy mandates, rigid latency budgets, or the need for iterative logical verification---dictate the orchestration method. Across the 54 primary studies, distinct architectural preferences emerge across four core applied domains.

In fields with strict privacy constraints (e.g., HIPAA compliance) or high-stakes accuracy requirements, hybridization is driven by the need to keep data local while grounding the SLM in verifiable facts. Consequently, local SLMs fine-tuned on clinical corpora and augmented by RAG are the dominant architecture (S17, S21). For example, S17 presents a hybrid framework for medical report generation that successfully improved patient readability across 6th to 18th-grade levels without losing clinical rigor by maintaining local detection. When evaluating radiology reports, S12 demonstrated that few-shot prompting allowed open-source models to statistically match the performance of massive models like GPT-4, achieving a Micro F1 score of 0.98 versus 0.97. This reliance on local ensembles and structured tabular transformations extends heavily into diagnostic classifications, ranging from herbal formulas to cardiovascular outcome probabilities (S19, S24). Distillation also proves highly effective in this domain; S35 showed that distilling knowledge from a massive 30B parameter model into a lightweight SLM yields a highly accurate, privacy-preserving tool for multilingual medical summarization. In complex scientific domains, such as molecular property prediction, S3 demonstrated that a 1.5B parameter DeepSeek model fused with Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) outperformed larger 7B and 8B variants, proving that local models can master highly specialized scientific vernacular when structurally augmented.

Tasks requiring rigid syntax, iterative refinement, and logical debugging benefit significantly from multi-agent or feedback-driven SLMs rather than zero-shot massive LLMs. In pedagogical text generation, S1 showed that a local 1.7B SLM orchestrated via a Markov Decision Process achieved a quality score of 4.17, which is directly comparable to the 4.15--4.31 range achieved by cloud-based LLMs. For code correction and database operations, S20 utilized specialized small models integrated with dual-scale memory systems to reduce computational redundancy by 26.4\% and semantic mistakes by 34.8\%. Automated assessment also benefits from structural orchestration, where highly structured report components allow smaller models to out-compete larger baselines (S23). Furthermore, orchestration frameworks frequently assign specific roles to SLMs to mimic the developer-reviewer dynamic and generate high-fidelity code offline (S13, S51, S52). S51 successfully utilized a three-agent system to generate bioinformatics code that approaches human-expert baselines, while S52 deployed a Feedback-Integrated Prompt Optimiser (FIPO) to iteratively revise SLM outputs based on explicit execution feedback. Finally, for code static analysis, S42 found that while massive LLMs demonstrate a clear advantage for broad type inference, smaller specialized models often suffice when integrated properly into the analysis pipeline.

Telecommunications architectures prioritize strict latency budgets, bandwidth allocation, and domain-specific vernacular, making hybrid edge-cloud systems the standard. The TeleOracle system evaluated in S4 demonstrated that an SLM generator could achieve an 81.20\% accuracy in telecom domain QA---a 30\% improvement over the base Phi-2 model---effectively matching GPT-4 while keeping data within telecom network constraints. This is reinforced by S44, which found that combining LoRA fine-tuning with a highly accurate ColBERT retrieval mechanism allows small models to effectively parse and answer highly technical telecom jargon. Beyond text generation, latency optimization and bandwidth reduction are critical drivers for hybridization (S29, S45, S53). S29 integrated lightweight sequence predictions with reinforcement learning to enable proactive bandwidth scheduling, significantly outperforming traditional reactive methods. Similarly, S45 and S53 utilized uncertainty-aware speculative inference and sub-task decomposition to dynamically bypass cloud LLMs, drastically reducing uplink transmissions and cutting average API costs by up to 83.57\%.

Real-world interactions require physical hardware constraints, such as CPU-only limitations or embedded edge devices, to govern model orchestration. In hybrid edge-cloud setups for complex reasoning, S2 emphasizes that only approximately 3\% of tokens are deemed ``hard'' enough to require cloud LLM verification, drastically saving edge compute resources. In mission-critical physical environments, distributed multi-agent architectures are relied upon to process raw operational data for geospatial and aerospace decision support (S11, S39). S11 highlights that locally embedded SLMs (e.g., Mistral 7B) can achieve accuracy comparable to cloud-based GPT-4o for specialized cockpit operations. In industrial video monitoring, S5 utilizes a dual-path hybrid framework to decouple visual information: the local SLM processes coarse-grained, uniform frames for rapid assessment, and only triggers the larger multimodal LLM to process keyframes when a complex, localized safety anomaly is detected. This modality-spanning orchestration extends into emerging cyber-physical tasks, such as cross-modal olfactory understanding in S41, where fine-tuned, heavily quantized multimodal SLMs process local sensor data to bridge the gap between physical inputs and language without remote computation.

\subsection{Cross-RQ Synthesis}
Viewed together, the four research questions suggest that the literature is converging toward a specific systems principle: hybrid language-model design works best when the large model is treated as a selectively invoked capability rather than the default engine for every token, query, or subtask.

The first implication is architectural. Strong results are associated with explicit control layers such as routers, planners, retrievers, or fusion modules. This means that future progress is unlikely to come only from making SLMs slightly larger or LLMs slightly cheaper. It will also depend on better confidence estimation, task decomposition, retrieval selection, and cross-model scheduling.

The second implication is methodological. Because accuracy dominates reporting, many studies overstate practical efficiency. The review already contains cases where stronger orchestration clearly reduces cost or latency, but it also contains cases where coordination shifts the bottleneck rather than removing it. A mature evaluation protocol should therefore report resource metrics as first-class outcomes rather than optional extras.

The third implication is application-oriented. The most successful SLM-centered deployments are not generic substitutes for LLMs. They are domain-adapted systems in which the small model is grounded, specialized, or protected by escalation logic. This is why telecom, structured reasoning, radiology labeling, optimisation formulation, and industrial monitoring are so prominent in the are so prominent in the positive findings of the review.

Beyond model selection, three core principles emerge: (1) \textbf{Design for routing}---orchestration, not model size, drives performance. Routing only 3--7\% of difficult tokens yields comparable results at lower cost (S2, S46).
